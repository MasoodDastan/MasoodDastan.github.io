Publications

  • Understanding the Links Between Firm Size, Exposure to Public Officials, and Firm Corruption Review of Development Economics, Under Review
    • This paper investigates the firm-level causes of corruption. Utilizing World Bank Enterprise Survey data from 68 countries, the study examines how firm characteristics influence the likelihood and severity of corruption, accounting for factors like institutional structure and public resource availability.
    • The research reveals that larger firm size and greater government influence over firms are positively associated with both the incidence and magnitude of corruption.
    • The study also finds that larger firms are more prone to engage in corrupt activities for potential growth benefits, whereas smaller firms may resort to bribery out of necessity. This underscores an asymmetrical relationship between corruption and firm size, emphasizing the need for differentiated anti-corruption strategies based on firm heterogeneity.
  • The effect of Corruption on Firm Investment in the Presence of Missing Fata American Journal of Economics and Sociology, 2023
    • The paper emphasizes the significance of nonresponse bias in firm-level studies on corruption. It finds that existing literature, which often disregards nonresponse bias, may overestimate the impact of corruption on firms. This overestimation occurs because many firms, especially in countries with less press freedom, tend not to report their experiences with corruption.
    • To address nonresponse bias, the paper employs a multiple imputation method to fill in missing data from the World Bank Enterprise Survey data. This approach allows for a more accurate analysis of the effect of corruption on firm investment decisions.
    • When nonresponse bias is accounted for using the multiple imputation method, the findings indicate that the negative relationship between corruption and firm investment is much smaller and statistically insignificant. This contrasts with previous studies that found a significant negative impact.
  • Electricity Consumption and Economic Growth in OPEC Countries: A Cointegrated Panel Analysis OPEC Energy Review, 2015
    • The paper examines the causal relationship between electricity consumption, economic growth, and trade activity in OPEC countries. Using panel cointegration and Error Correction Models (ECMs), it analyzes both short-run and long-run interactions between GDP, electricity usage, and trade, offering detailed insights into their interrelated dynamics
    • The study finds that GDP growth and trade significantly and positively influence electricity consumption, indicating a one-way causal effect from economic and trade expansion to increased electricity use.
    • The findings support the viability of energy conservation measures in OPEC countries without harming economic growth. It advocates for efficient energy utilization and diversifying energy sources beyond oil to spur economic development.
  • Analysis of Islamic Banks' Financing and Economic Growth: A Panel Cointegration Approach International Journal of Islamic and Middle Eastern Finance and Management, 2013
    • This study analyzes the effect of Islamic banking on economic growth in nine Islamic countries over 2000-2010 focusing on both short-term and long-term effects. Using panel cointegration methods, including unit root and Pedroni's long-run cointegration tests, it provides reliable insights into the bidirectional nature of the relationship between Islamic finance and economic growth.
    • study uncovers a two-way relationship between Islamic financing and economic growth. It also highlights that Islamic financing has a positive and significant impact on economic growth and capital accumulation in both the short and long term. These findings suggest that Islamic banking both stimulates and is affected by economic progress and plays a vital role as a financial intermediary.
    • The paper suggests that promoting Islamic banking can significantly benefit economic welfare. It recommends increasing the ratio of Islamic banking assets, encouraging more Islamic financial institutions, and revising regulations to support the growth of Islamic banking aligned with global trends.
